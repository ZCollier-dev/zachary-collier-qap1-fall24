Preface

The Bronco line of SUVs has had a very strange history, filled with several attempts to break into the off-road market from 1965 to today. This generation leans heavily into its heritage from back in the day. So, what makes this line's history so fascinating?

1965-1977

The first generation of Broncos is notable not only for being... well... the first, but also the longest-running Bronco generation to date. Time will tell if that holds true in the future.

Considered an indirect successor to WWII's Ford GPW, the Bronco was intended as a compliment to the new Ford Mustangs. Where the Mustangs are expensive sports cars, the Bronco was intended as a youth-friendly off-roader.

At the time, the Bronco offered up three body styles:

  • The Wagon - a 2-door vehicle with a removable hard top,
  • The Roadster - a roofless vehicle with inserts instead of doors, and
  • As a Sports Utility Pickup, or half-cab - replacing the rear bench seat for a mini pickup bed.

The Roadster lasted for 3 years, until 1968. The half-cab remained in production until 1973, leaving the Wagon as the sole body style up until the first generation's end in 1977.

Jim Allen from MOTORTREND in the article "The Bronco At 50: A History Of Ford's Trail Pony" writes this in reference to the production quantity of the first-generation Bronco: A total of 225,585 first-generation Broncos were built, according to the best available figures, made up of 203,544 wagons, 17,262 sports utility pickups, and 5,000 roadsters.

1978-1979

The gas crisis of 1973 caused manufacturers to cut costs wherever they could. Were it not for this, the Bronco of this generation may have been a 1974 or 1975 model instead. But alas, due to manufacturing difficulties and upcoming scheduled 1978 refresh of the Shrothorn project, the Bronco was the vehicle to get the facelift to save on tooling costs, featuring 2 doors only. The 4-door had to be cut short due to the risk.

Unlike its predecessors and progeny, which sported options for multiple engine sizes, the second generation only offered a V-8 engine. Despite its size, however, it underperformed in terms of horsepower.

In spite of the setbacks, the Bronco was a success. The published production for '78 is listed at 77,817 for both models. For '79, it looks like 48,274 Customs and 55,764 Ranger XLTs were built.Jim Allen from MOTORTREND in the article "The Bronco At 50: A History Of Ford's Trail Pony" writes.

1980-1986

One may suspect nothing to be changed from 2nd to 3rd generation Brocos, but under the hood, many changes were made to make the Bronco lighter. They also swapped the solid front axle for an Independent Front Suspension (IFS) called the Twin Traction Beam, making Ford the only American manufacturer to offer an IFS 4x4 at that time.

The engine also got an upgrade, now able to choose a V-6 option once more. The V-8 engine also got a power bump in 1982, and another significant bump with a 210 hp V-8 engine alongside a new 190 hp electric fuel injection V-8 engine.

The Ford Bronco II was released during this time to compete with the XJ Jeep Cherokee, a phenomenal vehicle at the time, along with the Chevrolet S-10 Blazer.

“The '80-'86 Bronco firmly established Ford in the growing SUV market, and around 325,000 were produced in this era, according to the best available numbers.” Jim Allen from MOTORTREND in the article "The Bronco At 50: A History Of Ford's Trail Pony"

1987-1991

The fourth generation Broncos were more focused on minor tweaks as opposed to full-on under-the-hood overhauls. Though, that does not mean there were no improvements, far from it. The introduction of an electric shift 4-wheel drive system into the vehicle, allowing the Bronco to have even better driving capabilities.

External restyling to make the Bronko look more modern and aerodynamic. The V-6 engine also recieved a power boost, jumping to 150 hp from 125 hp. Towing capacity hit a maximum of 7700 lbs during this generation - a value which it hovered at during the next generation as well.

In this generation's final year, 2500 Silver Anniversary vehicles were manufactured, painted a Currant Red, a plush, one-off leather interior in silver gray with captain's chairs. A sight to behold, I'm sure, and certainly the fourth generation's highlight.

With its upgrades and new look, just under 200,000 Broncos were manufactured during this time.

1992-1996

The fifth generation Broncos were the final generation for a long, long time. Airbags were introduced and made standard in the 1994 models, and a significant cosmetic refresh hoped to revitalize the Bronco. A Nite version of the Bronco XLT was added, featuring a blacked-out appearance and special badging. Very few were produced.

However, there was a Bronco killer in Ford's lineup - the 4-door Ford Explorer. Up until 1996, the Bronco's towing capacity allowed it to stick around. Eventually, the 1997 Expedition surpassed the capabilities of the Bronco in both towing capacity and fuel efficiency. With additional production ties to the mythical Ford F-150, the Bronco was laid to rest...

...

Or was it?

2020-Present

Around July 13th, 2020, the Ford Bronco and Ford Bronco Sport finally debuted. Times have changed a lot in the vehicle world since the last generation a whole 24 years later. The advancements in vehicle technology have been too numerous since then, so here are the most noteworthy changes.

Inspired by the retro aesthatic of Broncos past, the Bronco now has modern sensibilities, including now coming as a 4-door option in conjunction with the classic 2-door. Its looks are highly distinctive as a result of the retro-modern combo.

As with many vehicles nowadays, the new Broncos come with infotainment screens, between 8 and 12 inches. The Broncos also have removable doors and roofs, and much more powerful and efficient engine options compared to its predecessors. It also has Go Over Any Terrain modes, or GOAT modes. As the name implies, these modes allow the Bronco to adapt to any terrain at the press of a button. Makes sense, given goats' surprising ability to climb cliff faces in some instances.

But all this begs the question: why now? Interestingly, it landed smack-dab in the middle of the SUV craze and a rising interest in off-roading ventures. It's taking on the Jeep Wrangler, a line of SUVs which coasted along competitor free for a while as one of the top dogs of off-road vehicles, while also being the only SUV until the Bronco to have removable doors and roof.

With the Ford Bronco making a comeback, time will tell if the modern Bronco will continue to flourish, or if the rising cost of living all around will get the better of it in the end.
